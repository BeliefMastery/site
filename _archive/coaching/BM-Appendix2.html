Dependency in Human Dynamics – Needs-Based Taxonomy and Reference System

This section examines how outsourcing specific needs - across domains such as connection, well-being, honesty, peace, autonomy, and meaning - creates dependency and strains relationships, revealing the hidden toll of external reliance.


Unlike the narrative loop examples presented in Chapter 5, which are designed to evoke recognition through relational patterning, the entries here offer a clearer taxonomy and reference system. This framework mirrors the structure of the comprehensive needs vocabulary and is intended to support more precise navigation of the Belief Inference Process.


Grouped by need domain (e.g., Connection, Peace, Autonomy), each entry identifies a commonly outsourced need, the typical dependency structure it produces, the relational consequence that follows, and the core distortion that emerges over time.

This guide is designed for quick reference - to assist in identifying unmet needs beneath behavioral tension, classifying loop tendencies, and mapping projected consequences across personal inquiry or coaching sessions.


Use it to translate relational friction into needs-based language, or when tracing from a presenting behavior to the underlying unmet need driving it.

________________________________________

CONNECTION

Needs like acceptance, affection, appreciation, belonging, love, nurturing, respect, safety, security, stability, support, trust, warmth, and to know/be known or see/be seen are inherently relational and often sought from others. Outsourcing these can easily create dependency:

• Stability. An individual depends on another to be their emotional or practical anchor, outsourcing their sense of steadiness. The burdened party, feeling the weight of expectation, begins to waver, their own resilience compromised by the responsibility. As they falter, the seeker’s foundation collapses, and the provider, now unstable themselves, resents the demand. Stability erodes for both - one from dependence, the other from strain.


• Security. An individual looks to a partner for a sense of safety, placing the responsibility for their emotional or physical well-being in another’s hands. The partner, under constant pressure to provide reassurance, feels their own security compromised, struggling under the weight of expectation. The more they pull away, the more vulnerable the seeker becomes, amplifying the insecurity. In the end, both are left exposed - security vanishes for all.


• Consideration. An individual leans on a partner for constant reassurance that their feelings and perspectives matter, outsourcing their sense of being valued. The partner, burdened by the relentless need for validation, begins to feel unheard themselves, their own considerations neglected. Exhausted, they pull back, leaving the seeker feeling even more disregarded. What was meant to ensure mutual care instead breeds frustration - consideration erodes for both.


• Love and Affection. Someone clings to a partner, needing their love as proof of worth, outsourcing self-acceptance. The partner, feeling suffocated by the need, begins to withdraw, their own desire to give affection dulled by obligation. The seeker, sensing the distance, panics and grasps tighter, driving the cycle deeper. Love, meant to be freely given, dissolves under pressure - both are left cold and disconnected.


• Acceptance and Belonging. An individual relies on a group or partner to affirm their worth, bending to fit expectations in fear of rejection. The group, feeling the weight of their dependency, starts to distance themselves, resenting the responsibility of maintaining another’s identity. As the individual feels less accepted, they seek approval more desperately, feeding the alienation. What was sought - belonging - disintegrates, leaving all parties estranged.


• Trust. A person places all trust in another, outsourcing discernment and self-assurance. The trusted individual, knowing they bear the full weight of another’s faith, feels trapped - any mistake, any doubt, becomes catastrophic. Eventually, the strain erodes their reliability, and cracks appear. The seeker, sensing the instability, grows paranoid and doubtful, feeding cycles of betrayal. Trust collapses for both - the dependent feels deceived, the provider feels accused.

(Less dependency-prone needs include cooperation, communication, and mutuality, which imply reciprocity rather than one-sided reliance.)

PHYSICAL WELL-BEING

Most needs here - movement/exercise, sleep, sexual expression, touch, air, food, nutrition, rest, hydration, elimination, growth - are fundamentally individual, but outsourcing certain aspects can still create dynamics:

• Shelter and Safe-Space. An individual seeks refuge in a partner’s home or emotional presence, making them the sole source of comfort. The partner, overwhelmed by the responsibility of providing sanctuary, begins to feel burdened, their own sense of safety threatened by the imbalance. The more the seeker leans, the more the foundation buckles, leaving both ungrounded. What was meant to be a haven becomes a battleground - safety crumbles under the weight of dependence.


• Touch and Sexual Expression. One partner places the entirety of their physical intimacy needs on the other, depending on them for touch, connection, and validation. The other, feeling pressured rather than desired, begins to withdraw, their own relationship with intimacy strained by obligation. The seeker, feeling unwanted, clings harder, demanding more. Desire suffocates under expectation - neither finds fulfillment, and connection fades.


• Nourishment. A person depends on another to feed them - whether physically, emotionally, or spiritually - outsourcing the effort of self-sustenance. The provider, drained by the one-way exchange, begins to withhold, struggling to nourish themselves. The seeker, now deprived, grows desperate and demanding, accelerating the depletion. What was abundant now withers - neither is fed, and both are left hollow.

• Flow. A creative depends on a collaborator to drive momentum, outsourcing their own ability to enter a state of inspired productivity. The collaborator, weighed down by the responsibility, begins to falter, their own enthusiasm drained by the pressure to perform. Inspiration stagnates, progress halts, and tension builds - flow, once the goal, is now the casualty of dependence.

(Needs like air, sleep, or hydration are less relational and harder to outsource in a dependency sense, as they’re more self-managed.)

________________________________________

HONESTY (ELUCIDATION)

This category - authenticity, integrity, presence, joy, humor, fun, adventure - is largely internal, but outsourcing can still apply:

• Joy, Fun, and Adventure. An individual looks to a friend or partner as their source of excitement, placing the burden of spontaneity and happiness onto them. The provider, worn down by the expectation, loses their own enthusiasm, fun becoming a duty rather than a shared experience. The seeker, receiving less, pushes harder, making joy feel like a demand rather than a delight. In the end, both are drained - laughter fades, and adventure turns to obligation.


• Presence. A person expects another to be endlessly present for them, needing constant attention and validation. The provider, exhausted by the demand, begins to disengage, their own ability to be present fading under pressure. The seeker, sensing this, grows restless and insecure, clinging harder. Presence, once sought as connection, transforms into absence - neither feels truly seen.

(Integrity and authenticity are harder to outsource - they’re self-rooted - so dependency is less likely here.)

________________________________________

PEACE

• Beauty, Communion, Harmony, and Inspiration. Someone depends on a partner to create an atmosphere of peace, order, or creative energy, outsourcing their own engagement with these qualities. The partner, feeling responsible for sustaining the experience, begins to feel drained, their own sense of ease disrupted by duty. As they falter, the seeker grows anxious, grasping for the fading harmony. What was meant to be uplifting becomes exhausting - beauty, once abundant, now feels like a burden.


• Ease. An individual relies on a roommate to handle household responsibilities, outsourcing their sense of comfort and order. The roommate, feeling unappreciated and burdened by the imbalance, begins to withdraw their efforts, their own peace disrupted by resentment. As responsibilities go unmet, the seeker’s stress mounts, and the once-stable home environment fractures. What was meant to provide ease instead breeds tension - neither finds relief.

• Order. An individual relies on another to structure their world - be it a home, a schedule, or their thoughts. The provider, burdened by the endless responsibility, begins to resist, their own sense of order unraveling under the weight. The seeker, lost without guidance, grows more chaotic. What was meant to bring stability instead fosters disorder - both are left overwhelmed.


• Equality. A person expects another to constantly affirm their equal standing, demanding reassurance that they are valued and treated fairly. The provider, forced into the role of balancing perceptions, grows weary, resenting the obligation to monitor fairness. The more they pull away, the more the seeker feels diminished, demanding even more validation. What was meant to foster mutual respect breeds exhaustion - equality warps into imbalance.

________________________________________

AUTONOMY

Ironically, outsourcing autonomy-related needs - choice, freedom, independence, space, spontaneity - defeats their essence:

• Space or Freedom. Someone depends on another to grant them permission for independence, making their autonomy subject to another’s will. The provider, feeling the burden of controlling another’s freedom, becomes frustrated and resentful, feeling trapped by their role as gatekeeper. The more control they hold, the more confined they feel. What was meant to create space instead breeds suffocation - both are left constrained.

________________________________________

MEANING

This category - awareness, celebration of life, challenge, clarity, competence, contribution, creativity, growth, hope, learning, purpose, self-expression, understanding - is rich with internal potential, but some needs invite dependency:

• Approval, Being Liked, Being Loved, Being Esteemed, Being Wanted. An individual relies on external validation to feel worthy, outsourcing self-respect. The provider, overwhelmed by the responsibility of maintaining another’s self-esteem, withdraws, their appreciation dulled by obligation. The seeker, feeling less seen, becomes more desperate, accelerating the erosion. What was once freely given becomes a burden - esteem vanishes for both.


• Mourning. An individual leans on friends to carry them through grief, outsourcing their ability to process loss. The friends, initially supportive, begin to feel overwhelmed, their own emotional reserves depleted by the weight of another’s sorrow. Unable to sustain the dynamic, they withdraw, leaving the seeker feeling abandoned. What was meant to foster healing instead deepens isolation - mourning becomes stunted for all involved.


• Trust in Something Greater. An individual places their faith in another - be it a partner, leader, or guru - as the foundation of their belief system. The provider, now burdened with maintaining another’s existential security, begins to doubt themselves, their own faith cracking under pressure. When their stability falters, the seeker’s world collapses. What was meant to provide meaning instead fosters disillusionment - both are left unmoored.

(Needs like companionship, creativity, awareness, or purpose are less dependency-prone when kept internal, though outsourcing them (e.g., needing others to define your purpose) could still strain relationships.)

Glossary of Vice

(The term “vice” is not used here in a moralistic sense. It refers to emotional coping postures - habitual, often subconscious, affective responses to unmet needs. These patterns are not inherently wrong - they are protective, but unsustainable. This vocabulary allows precision when naming defensive emotional strategies.)

Vice Definition

Anger Intense displeasure or irritation in response to perceived injustice, insult, or frustration.

Anguish Severe emotional distress, often from prolonged suffering or loss.

Animosity Strong hostility or active dislike toward others.

Anxiety Persistent worry, tension, or unease, often about uncertain outcomes or potential threats.

Avarice Extreme greed for material wealth or gain, often at the expense of others.

Bitterness Resentful or cynical feelings stemming from perceived unfair treatment or unmet expectations.

Desperation A state of intense need or hopelessness that leads to irrational or extreme actions.

Disappointment Sadness or dissatisfaction from unmet expectations or goals.

Disgust Strong aversion or repulsion, often toward something offensive or morally wrong.

Elitism A belief in or behavior that favors a perceived superior group over others.

Envy Resentful longing for others' qualities, achievements, or possessions.

Fear Intense emotional response to perceived danger, threat, or harm.

Frustration Feelings of annoyance and helplessness from unmet goals or obstructed efforts.

Gluttony Overindulgence in or excessive consumption of food, wealth, or other resources.

Greed Intense desire for more than one needs or deserves, often concerning wealth, status, or power.

Guilt A sense of remorse or responsibility for perceived wrongs or mistakes.

Hate Extreme aversion, animosity, or hostility toward a person, group, or concept.

Impatience Intolerance of delay, opposition, or others’ needs, often leading to irritability.

Inferiority Feelings of inadequacy or low self-worth in comparison to others.

Intolerance Inability or unwillingness to accept differing views, practices, or people.

Lust Intense, often obsessive, desire for physical gratification or pleasure.

Masochism The tendency to derive pleasure from one's own suffering, pain, or humiliation. Often takes the form of a belief that nature is here to hurt you, or that suffering is right.

Rage/Outrage Uncontrolled anger or wrath, often with an urge to take destructive action. To be enraged is to be consumed by a storm of anger that overrides reason, typically tied to a personal wound or offense. To be outraged is to experience indignation grounded in moral reasoning, often tied to a broader cause or value system.

Resentment Lingering negative feelings toward someone or something perceived as unfair or unjust.

Shame Painful feelings of humiliation or worthlessness due to perceived personal failings.

Sloth Reluctance to exert effort or laziness, especially in responsibilities or duties.

Worthlessness A sense of lacking value, significance, or usefulness.

Wrath Intense anger with a strong desire for retribution or punishment.

Belief Inference Process (Concise Guide)

Step 1: Surface the Pattern

Describe a recurring situation in your life that causes frustration, exhaustion, or quiet despair - or one where you're compulsively attached to a role or behavior despite its cost.

Look for subtle gleeful reactions in inappropriate contexts or overly “noble” roles that secretly drain you.

🔧 Ask yourself:

• What do I keep doing even when it hurts?

• What roles would collapse if I stopped performing them?

________________________________________

Step 2: Identify the Vice Response

What reactive or ‘negative’ emotional response arises in these moments? Is it anger, shame, fear, bitterness, resentment, or collapse?

This reaction is your protection strategy - a vice designed to shield you from unbearable emotional exposure.

🔧 Clarify:

Is it active (rage, control)? Passive (withdrawal, coldness)?

Does it isolate you or make connection harder?

________________________________________

Step 3: Determine the Aggravated Need

What are you trying to get, avoid, or protect in this situation? Use the Needs list.

Is this need showing up as compulsion (you chase it), aversion (you avoid it), or dependency (you outsource it)?

🔧 Key Insight:

This is not just a missing need - it’s a distorted need. It's been overburdened with meaning or denied so often that you can't relate to it cleanly anymore.

________________________________________

Step 4: Recall the Earliest Memory of This Pattern

What’s the first time you remember this dynamic happening?

Who was involved, and what was the emotional climate?

🔧 Look for:

• What you adapted to survive.

• Early emotional atmospheres that felt confusing, unsafe, or invisible.

________________________________________

Step 5: Identify the Exiled Emotion

What unbearable emotional state did you experience in that early moment - so painful that you vowed (often subconsciously) never to feel it again?

🔧 This is the emotion your vice (Step 2) is designed to suppress.

Feelings like shame, helplessness, guilt, or abandonment often anchor the whole pattern.

________________________________________

Step 6: Identify the Original Unmet Need

What was most missing in that early moment?

What care, recognition, or emotional nourishment would have made the scene safe or coherent?

🔧 This is the root unmet need - the one your belief formed to protect.

You likely abandoned, over-performed for, or outsourced this need ever since.

________________________________________

Step 7: Trace the Origin Pattern Logic

What did your environment consistently teach you - implicitly or explicitly - about this need?

🔧 Ask:

“What would I have had to believe to stay safe or sane in that environment?”

This isn’t the belief itself yet - just the internal logic that made it necessary.

________________________________________

Step 8: Infer the Core Belief

Write the subconscious belief you formed to explain and protect against the pain.

“I must...”, “People are...”, “The world is...” etc.

🔧 Remember:

Beliefs are not just wrong ideas. They are emotional protection contracts - adaptive, not logical.

They helped you survive - but now they distort reality and restrict your options.

________________________________________

Step 9: Validate the Belief via Behavior

How do your compulsions and aversions (from Steps 1–3) support this belief?

🔧 Check:

• Do you avoid situations that could contradict it?

• Does your behavior ensure the belief is never tested - or always confirmed?

________________________________________

Step 10: Validate the Belief via Resistance

Now flip the belief. Say the opposite out loud.

What happens inside you?

🔧 Track your internal resistance:

• Cynical doubt: “Yeah right/that’s naive.”

• Skeptical doubt: “No evidence/where’s the proof?”

• External blame: “Other people are the problem.”

• Harsh self-attack: “This is what’s wrong with you.”

These voices are the belief’s immune system - defenses against contradiction.

________________________________________

Step 11: Identify the Denied Expression

What part of you had to stay hidden because of the belief?

What couldn’t you do, say, become, or express?

🔧 Imagine a character following your belief on a stage:

What do they avoid?

What traits do they suppress?

This reveals the lost potential - the qualities most needing reclamation.

________________________________________

Step 12: Identify the Exposed Sensitivities

Now that the belief is seen, it no longer shields you.

Expect hypersensitivity around the original need (Step 6), the exiled emotion (Step 5), or the denied expression (Step 11).

Prepare for reactivity. Don’t pathologize it.

Instead, plan gentle exposure, contradiction strategies, and emotional resourcing.

Final Insight:

The process doesn’t just remove a belief - it removes the armor that kept you safe.

Make room for the tenderness underneath. And stay with it.
