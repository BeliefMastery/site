1. Appendix 3. Tactical Manipulation Compendium



A Field Manual for Navigating Coercion, Control, and Psychological Sabotage


    1. Introduction: A Sword and a Scabbard

This appendix is a diagnostic weapon. What follows is a structured compendium of 63 manipulation tactics - distilled, categorized, and sequenced to expose how personal and interpersonal sovereignty is compromised.

These patterns are not presented for moral scrutiny -they are included because they work; each tactic is a structure: a reproducible pattern that shows how power is extracted, how agency is overridden, and how the will is bent (sometimes subtly, sometimes explicitly). It is not an opportunity to sharpen your ability to control.

You will find tactics drawn from six core manipulation vectors - fear, dependency, deception, obsession, adoration, and sexual exploitation - as introduced in Chapter 5. Here, they are rendered granular. Practical. Traceable. Named, mapped, and contextualized for sovereign application.

Each entry includes:

  • Example: A typical expression of the pattern

  • Mechanism: The underlying structure or strategy

  • Leverage: How the tactic secures power or compliance

  • Consequences: For both perpetrator and victim

  • Mode: Whether the tactic is typically Covert, Overt, or Contextual

  • Phase: Whether it operates at Ingress (entry), Entrenchment (control), or Extraction (harvest)

These distinctions matter. Without them, clarity is flattened into blame or abstraction.


This appendix is designed as an Integrity Tool. Whether you're reviewing a past relationship, interrogating a current one, or confronting your own behavioral patterns, this compendium will surface what is often hidden:

  • Leverage masked as care

  • Control framed as protection

  • Dominance disguised as devotion

After this point, you cannot unsee how manipulation operates. You cannot protect your illusions with ignorance. The clarity offered here will follow you into conflict, into intimacy, into negotiation, into silence. If wielded without restraint, it can damage more than it reveals. Understanding how manipulation works is not the same as becoming sovereign. In fact, knowledge without integrity can accelerate distortion. The sharpest insights, when applied without structure, become tactics of control.

To navigate this section responsibly, you must understand the dual nature of power:

  • The sword reveals distortion. It allows you to cut through confusion and sever covert control.

  • The scabbard is restraint. It ensures that discernment does not devolve into domination.

These tools are powerful. They expose what many systems deliberately obscure, but exposure alone is not growth and awareness without responsibility becomes justification.


Many of these tactics will feel familiar. You will recognize how others have used them. You may also recognize how you have used them yourself - consciously or unconsciously - and here lies the temptation:

  • To use this awareness to win, not to liberate.

  • To analyze others while protecting your own distortions.

  • To justify strategic manipulation as necessary, noble, or deserved.

Manipulation always extracts a price - first from the other, then from the self. It may grant temporary control, but it erodes trust, collapses intimacy, and replaces contact with compliance. Every shortcut comes with a cost.

After this point, you can no longer claim you didn’t know how manipulation operates. You cannot pretend your motives were clean if your methods were not. It will follow you into conflict, into attraction, into negotiation, into silence. It will become part of your lens - and if you’re not careful, part of your arsenal.

You are free to use this tool, but if you do so without restraint, context, or self-reflection - then what you build will not be sovereignty.


    1. Fear-Based Exploitation (Tactics #1–6)

1. Coercive Fragility (Covert Ingress)

Example: “I’ll break when I break… I don’t really care anymore… I’ll keep going until I break.”

Mechanism: Presents emotional collapse as inevitable, subtly threatening emotional damage if unmet needs are named.

Leverage: Triggers the partner’s compassion reflex, weaponizing empathy to block confrontation or distance.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Secures emotional shielding through strategic helplessness but erodes relational credibility. Over time, becomes incapable of receiving direct truth or being seen without collapse framing. Emotional authority is replaced with theatrical control, and internal growth is arrested by the chronic performance of suffering.

Consequence for Victim: Will is suspended to prevent their breakdown. Emotional truth becomes dangerous, and boundaries become abandonment.


2. Threat Saturation (Overt Ingress)

Example: “I’ll just leave right now, and you’ll never see me again.”

Mechanism: Floods the relational space with repeated threats - of abandonment, collapse, or retribution - until the nervous system remains in survival mode.

Leverage: Uses unpredictability and fear of loss to hijack emotional coherence and enforce compliance.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Installs control through volatility, but loses access to authentic mirroring. Eventually becomes surrounded only by compliance and suppression. Trust becomes impossible, even if desired, and paranoia grows where relational certainty should have lived. All emotional signals become filtered through the lens of anticipated abandonment, creating a cycle of self-fulfilling fear.

Consequence for Victim: Becomes hypervigilant, suppresses authenticity to avoid collapse, and experiences chronic relational anxiety.


3. Manufactured Crises (Covert Entrench)

Example: Softens at the end of a walk only to reignite conflict over lunch.

Mechanism: Introduces destabilizing conflict or drama at key emotional moments to interrupt peace and clarity.

Leverage: Keeps the partner off balance by triggering repeated survival-mode engagement.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Becomes emotionally dependent on chaos to feel significance. Intimacy becomes intolerable unless disrupted. Over time, loses capacity for stillness, and genuine connection feels hollow or threatening. The nervous system adapts to conflict as the only source of vitality, making peace feel like irrelevance.

Consequence for Victim: Intimacy becomes conditional. Loses access to spontaneity and develops performance anxiety around relational safety.


4. Despair as a Cage (Covert Entrench)

Example: “If I break, you won’t have to deal with me anymore.”

Mechanism: Leverages despair as emotional ransom, framing separation or boundary-setting as an act of cruelty.

Leverage: Uses hopelessness as a containment tool, evoking dread of triggering deeper collapse.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Avoids emotional maturation by converting pain into leverage. Dignity is traded for pity. Over time, becomes reliant on being perceived as broken to maintain attachment, thereby losing the capacity to be held in strength or truth. Emotional stagnation becomes identity.

Consequence for Victim: Becomes a crisis manager rather than a partner. Compassion becomes duty. Boundaries equate to abandonment.


5. Safety Hoarding (Overt Entrench)

Example: “If you talk to your friends about this, they’ll just manipulate you too.”

Mechanism: Frames all external options or perspectives as dangerous or invalid, positioning the manipulator as the only safe haven.

Leverage: Uses isolation and implied threats to keep the partner dependent on a single relational source of perceived safety.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Gains total narrative control but loses relational diversity and external calibration. Becomes trapped in their own projected authority, unable to receive challenge or contrast. Trust becomes impossible - both of others and of oneself - and intimacy decays into echo-chamber dominance.

Consequence for Victim: Becomes isolated and confused; trust in their own discernment erodes and internal clarity collapses.


6. Relational Blackmail (Both Covert and Overt Entrench)

Example: “If you see me this way, then this isn’t love.”

Mechanism: Forces the partner to choose between emotional honesty and continued connection.

Leverage: Reframes truth as betrayal, turning discernment into a threat against the relationship.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Redefines love as submission, securing temporary emotional power but severing the possibility of being truly known. All disagreement becomes betrayal, which isolates the self from authentic feedback. Over time, loses the capacity to distinguish reverence from compliance, and collapses into emotional delusion.

Consequence for Victim: Must trade clarity for connection. Emotional truth becomes incompatible with love.

    1. Dependency & Control-Based Exploitation (Tactics #7–12)

7. Resource Withholding (Overt Extract)

Example: “You don’t contribute financially, so your opinion doesn’t matter.”

Mechanism: Uses control of money, shelter, or other essential resources to limit autonomy and silence dissent.

Leverage: Creates a dependency hierarchy where survival or comfort becomes conditional on compliance.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Secures decision-making authority through material control, but at the cost of relational legitimacy. Over time, becomes incapable of receiving disagreement without resorting to threat. Trust in their ability to relate without leverage decays, and affection becomes indistinguishable from obligation. The relationship’s vitality collapses under transactional dominance.

Consequence for Victim: Internalizes unworthiness; feels trapped and voiceless; suppresses needs to avoid being cut off.


8. Manufactured Helplessness (Covert Entrench)

Example: “I just don’t know how to manage that stuff - you’re so much better at it.”

Mechanism: Feigns incompetence, forgetfulness, or distress to push responsibility for planning, labor, or conflict resolution onto the partner.

Leverage: Guilt and caretaking instincts are triggered, making the partner feel obligated to manage both emotional and logistical burdens.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Avoids direct confrontation and adult responsibility by collapsing into passive dependence. Over time, loses core competencies and erodes self-trust. Becomes relationally infantile - unable to contribute without manipulation. Long-term partnership devolves into disguised codependence, hollowed out by chronic imbalance.

Consequence for Victim: Becomes over-functioning, exhausted, and confused about the boundaries of care vs. servitude.


9. Dependency Grooming (Covert Entrench)

Example: Gradually undermines friendships, discourages work, or critiques independence as “unfeminine” or “unsafe.”

Mechanism: Dismantles external support structures and self-confidence through sustained subtle discouragement or sabotage.

Leverage: Long-term emotional and logistical dependency ensures control over the partner’s choices, movement, and self-image.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Establishes long-term dominance by dismantling the partner’s independence, but loses access to authentic connection. The partner becomes a reflection of captivity, not choice. Over time, the manipulator fears the partner’s freedom because it threatens the control scaffold. Intimacy becomes surveillance, and trust is replaced by possessive reinforcement.

Consequence for Victim: Loses reference points, external mirrors, and capacity for independent action; autonomy collapses.


10. Emotional Debt Traps (Covert Entrench)

Example: “After everything I’ve done for you, you’re really going to act like this?”

Mechanism: Care, support, or sacrifice is offered with hidden emotional conditions - unacknowledged strings later pulled to extract obedience.

Leverage: Weaponizes gratitude to distort reciprocity; turns kindness into leverage.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Disguises coercion as sacrifice, but corrupts the meaning of care in the process. Emotional giving becomes transactional, and real generosity atrophies. Over time, the manipulator becomes addicted to moral high ground and incapable of clean offering. They construct a self-image as martyr, but remain unseen and resented.

Consequence for Victim: Feels guilt for asserting boundaries; associates love with unpayable emotional debt.


11. Scarcity Positioning (Covert Entrench)

Example: “No one else would love you the way I do.”

Mechanism: Undermines the partner’s belief in their worth or options, framing the relationship as their best or only hope for love and support.

Leverage: Insecurity and low self-esteem are reinforced to maintain emotional captivity.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Maintains control by engineering dependence, but fractures the mirror of love. Praise and presence are no longer freely given - they are currency to be hoarded. Over time, self-worth becomes tethered to being needed rather than being chosen. The manipulator begins to fear abandonment, not because they love, but because their identity cannot exist outside the imbalance.

Consequence for Victim: Begins to see self as unlovable or dependent; fears leaving even harmful situations.


12. Isolation via Need Inflation (Covert Entrench)

Example: “I just need you so much - it breaks me when you’re not around.”

Mechanism: Inflates emotional need or attachment language to frame all external relationships as threatening or disloyal.

Leverage: Encourages withdrawal from community or friendships under the guise of love, intimacy, or deep need.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Uses exaggerated emotional need to monopolize attention, but destroys relational diversity in the process. Becomes incapable of regulating their own nervous system without external anchoring. Over time, intimacy mutates into emotional surveillance, and the partner’s autonomy is experienced as threat. Real connection becomes suffocation masked as closeness.

Consequence for Victim: Suffocates under responsibility for another’s emotional state; loses relational diversity and perspective.

    1. Deception & Illusion-Based Exploitation (Tactics #13–18)

13. Gaslighting (Overt and Covert Entrench)

Example: “That never happened - you’re imagining things.”

Mechanism: Systematically denies, reframes, or distorts shared events to undermine the partner’s memory, perception, or emotional response.

Leverage: Replaces objective reality with a narrative controlled by the manipulator, making the victim reliant on them for ‘truth.’

Consequence for Perpetrator: Maintains narrative supremacy, but loses access to truth calibration. Over time, begins to believe their own distortions, collapsing the internal distinction between honesty and strategy. The manipulator’s sense of reality fractures beneath its own edits, resulting in epistemic isolation - where only domination feels coherent.

Consequence for Victim: Doubts memory, intuition, and logic; experiences psychological disorientation and eventual self-erasure.


14. Mirror Masking (Covert Ingress)

Example: “You’re unlike anyone I’ve ever met - so pure, so special.” (followed weeks later by rejection or devaluation)

Mechanism: Reflects an idealized projection of the partner’s self back to them, accelerating attachment through simulated resonance.

Leverage: Forges intimacy based on illusion, setting up a later collapse or reversal to gain further control.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Generates rapid attachment by simulating resonance, but forfeits the possibility of being truly seen. Over time, becomes emotionally dislocated - only able to engage through projection, not presence. When the illusion collapses, they are left with no access to intimacy - only a pattern of seduction, disillusionment, and discard.

Consequence for Victim: Bonds to a false mirror image of self; feels betrayed and hollow when devaluation replaces idealization.


15. Benevolent Facade (Covert Ingress)

Example: “I only check your phone because I care so much - it’s for your own good.”

Mechanism: Masks control tactics as acts of protection, devotion, or responsibility.

Leverage: Secures compliance under the illusion of concern; evokes gratitude instead of resistance.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Secures obedience through the aesthetics of care, but severs the possibility of trust. Over time, becomes addicted to appearing good rather than being real. Control becomes more important than connection. The manipulator’s internal coherence fragments, as they increasingly confuse moral image with moral reality.

Consequence for Victim: Becomes confused between control and care; unable to detect coercion within affection.


16. Implanted Motives (Covert Entrench)

Example: “You’ve always wanted a quiet life, haven’t you?” (after discouraging ambition or movement)

Mechanism: Inserts ideas, desires, or identities into the partner’s psyche through suggestion, repetition, or subtle praise.

Leverage: Makes the partner believe imposed limitations or choices are self-generated.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Avoids overt manipulation, but becomes incapable of relating without covert influence. Over time, begins to distrust genuine choice in others, assuming all agency is programmable. Authentic partnership becomes intolerable, as it threatens the constructed architecture of control. They lose faith in others’ sovereignty because they’ve erased it too many times.

Consequence for Victim: Internal reality is subtly rewritten; loses connection to authentic preference and will.


17. Reality Rewriting (Overt Entrench)

Example: “You never supported me - this has always been one-sided.” (after years of evidence to the contrary)

Mechanism: Retroactively reframes past events to invalidate the partner’s contributions and recenter the narrative around injury or betrayal.

Leverage: Erases inconvenient history and rewrites memory to gain present moral leverage.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Escapes consequence by altering memory, but destroys continuity of self in the process. Over time, must maintain an increasingly complex distortion field to preserve the false narrative. Eventually, collapses into either paranoia or delusion, unable to track what was real, what was false, or why anyone trusted them to begin with.

Consequence for Victim: Begins to doubt past intentions and actions; identity destabilizes under narrative distortion.


18. Sympathy Baiting (Covert Entrench)

Example: “After everything I’ve survived, I just can’t believe you’d say that to me.”

Mechanism: Invokes past suffering or trauma to disarm criticism, interrupt accountability, or pre-empt confrontation.

Leverage: Turns feedback into perceived cruelty; hijacks the moral frame.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Short-circuits accountability by weaponizing pain, but corrodes credibility with each deployment. Over time, others begin to withhold truth out of pity or fatigue. The manipulator loses access to clean reflection, becomes insulated from consequence, and ultimately imprisons themselves in a victim identity too fragile to receive growth.

Consequence for Victim: Feels guilt for naming harm; emotional discernment is bypassed in favor of pity.


    1. Obsession-Based Exploitation (Tactics #19–24)

19. Drama Looping (Overt or Covert Entrench)

Example: Regular cycles of escalating arguments, followed by reconciliations that never resolve root issues.

Mechanism: Sustains emotional chaos through repeated conflict and incomplete repair, keeping the nervous system on edge.

Leverage: Creates a closed feedback loop where emotional regulation depends on re-entering the drama cycle.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Maintains intensity by manufacturing volatility, but loses access to sustainable intimacy. The nervous system adapts to conflict as connection, making peace feel dead and resolution intolerable. Over time, becomes dependent on chaos for meaning, unable to distinguish love from crisis.

Consequence for Victim: Becomes addicted to emotional highs and lows; loses the ability to recognize calm as safe or real.


20. Obsession Seeding (Covert Ingress)

Example: Intense early intimacy followed by cold withdrawal, then breadcrumbing with suggestive attention.

Mechanism: Cycles unpredictably between warmth and absence to induce fixation and pursuit.

Leverage: Keeps the partner guessing, searching, and reaching for connection under false promise of depth.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Avoids vulnerability while ensuring ongoing attention, but forfeits the capacity to receive love not distorted by chase or confusion. Over time, becomes emotionally untouchable - only able to function in dynamics of seduction and distance. Intimacy becomes a game of proximity, never presence.

Consequence for Victim: Emotional bandwidth consumed by longing, rumination, and imagined potential.


21. Artificial Urgency (Overt Ingress)

Example: “If you can’t decide by tonight, I’m walking away forever.”

Mechanism: Creates false time pressure to override reflection and force reactive decisions.

Leverage: Hijacks the nervous system into fight-or-flight, bypassing discernment.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Secures outcomes by collapsing the decision window, but erodes relational trust and long-term stability. Over time, others stop bringing important decisions to them. Life becomes filled with artificial crisis, and real urgency is no longer distinguishable from manipulation.

Consequence for Victim: Makes irreversible choices under duress; confuses intensity with importance.


22. Emotional Enmeshment (Covert Entrench)

Example: “If you’re upset, I feel destroyed. If you need space, I can’t breathe.”

Mechanism: Merges emotional states and identity into a single, shared field where autonomy becomes abandonment.

Leverage: Uses fusion to make emotional separation feel like moral betrayal.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Secures emotional centrality by merging identities, but becomes incapable of self-regulation without control. Over time, becomes hypersensitive to separation and begins to interpret autonomy as abandonment. All space becomes threat, and intimacy becomes a demand, not a gift.

Consequence for Victim: Identity diffuses; loses clarity on where they end and the other begins.


23. Fixation Farming (Covert Entrench)

Example: Alternates between deep attention and subtle withholding to keep the partner “earning” affection.

Mechanism: Feeds just enough affection, hope, or progress to keep the partner seeking further validation.

Leverage: Keeps the partner engaged in solving a puzzle that is never meant to resolve.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Retains power through engineered pursuit, but becomes invisible behind the puzzle. Others bond to the chase - not to them. Over time, begins to fear stillness and honesty, believing that without ambiguity, they offer nothing. Intimacy is replaced by performative scarcity.

Consequence for Victim: Entrenched in addictive cycles of hope and effort; identity becomes tied to “earning” love.


24. Relational Eclipse (Overt or Covert Entrench)

Example: “You don’t need anyone else - you have me.”

Mechanism: Absorbs the partner’s full relational bandwidth, framing all external connections as unsafe or less meaningful.

Leverage: Becomes the sole source of emotional, social, and spiritual fulfillment - then weaponizes that position.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Controls the relational ecosystem completely, but becomes imprisoned in the role of sole sustainer. Over time, collapses under the pressure of being everything. The partner’s individuality becomes intolerable, and freedom is experienced as betrayal. No relationship survives that gravity without burning.

Consequence for Victim: Feels guilty or disloyal for having independent friendships, interests, or emotional needs.

Consequence for Victim: Feels like a failure within success; over-adapts to regain approval, loses access to authentic expression.

Over time, becomes dependent on admiration to feel secure, and begins to fear being seen without myth. Vulnerability becomes a threat to the exalted image, and the self disappears behind the role.

30. Emotional Investment Extraction (Covert Extract)

Example: “You know I need you - you’re the only one who really believes in me.” (while demanding time, silence, or work)

Leverage: Makes love a resource to be harvested rather than reciprocated.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Harvests love as a resource, but becomes emotionally bankrupt. Over time, cannot receive care without extracting value. Affection feels transactional, and relationship becomes a one-way conduit of supply. The partner’s devotion is no longer love - it is maintenance of the manipulator’s self-concept.

Consequence for Victim: Feels trapped by their own depth of feeling; love becomes synonymous with servitude.

Consequence for Perpetrator: Gains access through ambiguity, but corrupts the foundation of trust and relational memory. Over time, loses the ability to distinguish between mutuality and override. All future connection is laced with doubt - both theirs and others. Their capacity to be received in love collapses beneath the weight of exploited consent.

Consequence for Victim: May feel complicit in their own violation; trauma is compounded by internal confusion.


    1. Domination-Based Exploitation (Tactics #37–51)

Overt control patterns that assert dominance through intimidation, disruption, or suppression of autonomy.

37. Volumetric Override (Overt Entrench)
Example: A partner yells over any attempt at response.
Mechanism: Uses loudness and interruption to overwhelm the partner’s nervous system.
Leverage: Shocks the other into submission by derailing cognitive clarity.
Consequence for Perpetrator: Secures verbal dominance through sheer intensity, but collapses the possibility of dialogue. Over time, others stop sharing truth entirely. The manipulator becomes surrounded by silence and resentment - mistaking fear-based compliance for respect, and volume for understanding.
Consequence for Victim: Self-censorship, nervous system tension, chronic fear of verbal engagement.


38. Binary Ultimatum Trap (Overt Entrench)
Example: “If you keep talking to him, we’re done.”
Mechanism: Compresses dialogue into a lose-lose decision.
Leverage: Forces concession under threat of relational collapse.
Consequence for Perpetrator: Forces decisions through threat, but erodes any sense of mutual authorship. Over time, becomes incapable of compromise or relational nuance. Every disagreement becomes a standoff, and connection is replaced by conditional loyalty under threat of abandonment.
Consequence for Victim: Develops false-self adaptation; equates love with self-erasure.


39. Postural Threat Induction (Overt Ingress)
Example: Standing over a seated partner during conflict.
Mechanism: Uses physical positioning to invoke unspoken threat.
Leverage: Triggers freeze/fawn responses that disable verbal autonomy.
Consequence for Perpetrator: Gains short-term compliance through bodily dominance, but embeds fear into the relational field. Over time, presence becomes associated with danger, not support. The body is no longer trusted as a vehicle for safety, and true intimacy is replaced by avoidance or shutdown.
Consequence for Victim: Feels physically unsafe; internalizes relational fear as norm.


40. Artificial Constraint Framing (Overt Entrench)
Example: “You have 30 seconds to explain yourself.”
Mechanism: Sets arbitrary time limits to suppress nuance.
Leverage: Controls the pace and scope of emotional truth.
Consequence for Perpetrator: Controls the frame by restricting time or expression, but trains others to conceal or rush their truth. Over time, loses access to full emotional information. Becomes increasingly intolerant of nuance, and emotionally starved by the very silence they enforce.
Consequence for Victim: Induced disorientation and perceived incompetence.


41. Spectacle Reframe (Overt Entrench)
Example: “Tell your friends what you just said.”

    1. Conclusion

Every tactic in this compendium is a variation of the same underlying strategy: To bypass mutual sovereignty by outsourcing regulation - without appearing to do so.

Some use threat, others use affection. Some feign collapse, others provoke guilt or confusion. The core remains unchanged: Control without ownership, influence without exposure, and safety through distortion.

When these tactics are active, the relationship no longer functions as a meeting of equals. One partner becomes a service provider; managing another’s pain, projection, or volatility. Boundaries become threats, honesty becomes danger, and clarity becomes betrayal. Over time, this dynamic corrodes both participants. The one who manipulates loses the capacity for clean, earned connection, and the one being manipulated begins to confuse appeasement with love. In either case, sovereignty is traded - not for connection, but for control masked as care.


The Antidote Is Structural

There is no verbal trick that resolves manipulation. There is no clever line that disentangles these tactics when they are embedded. The antidote is not aesthetic - it is structural:

  • Uncompromising clarity in your perception and standards. Not cruelty. Not callout. Simply truth that does not bend to fear or performance.

  • Refusal to reward distortion - not by retaliating, but by refusing to subsidize it with attention, compliance, or self-erasure.

  • Non-negotiable boundaries around communication, consent, and relational rhythm. Not ultimatums. Not withdrawal. Just clear containers where sovereignty is the baseline for participation.

This is about moral superiority and signal integrity. It is about becoming impossible to confuse - because you decide to be the one that decides when you sacrifice for others.

Let this appendix serve as both map and mirror. Now that you’ve seen the patterns, the next step is confrontation and authorship. Either you are sovereign, or you losing a negotiation.
