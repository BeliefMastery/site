Appendix 1. Patterns Compendium - Human Coping Strategies


Effectively navigating coping patterns using a structured approach: Pattern, Impact, Needs, Compulsions, Aversions, Triggers, Vice Expression



Introduction to Coping Strategies

Unmet needs shape our beliefs, and these beliefs, in turn, dictate our behaviors, emotional responses, and worldview. This chapter expands on that foundation by systematically breaking down readily recognizable coping mechanisms - common patterns that emerge as responses to unfulfilled needs.

Rather than simply identifying these patterns, this compendium provides a structured framework for analysis. Each pattern is dissected into key components: the unmet needs driving it, the compulsions and aversions it creates, the triggers that activate it, and the vice expressions that reinforce it. This structure makes it easy to transition from recognizing a pattern to understanding the needs it compensates for - offering a direct path to resolution.

Reactive coping mechanisms undermine autonomy by binding choices to unconscious conditioning rather than intentional action. True self-authorship requires breaking these cycles. By systematically deconstructing these patterns, we gain leverage over them, reclaiming control over our responses and reinforcing sovereignty over our own minds.

________________________________________

A Framework for Recognizing Patterns

Coping mechanisms follow predictable structures, shaped by unmet needs and reinforced through repeated behavior. To systematically analyze these patterns, this framework dissects them into core components, allowing for precise identification and intervention.

Each pattern is broken down into the following elements:

• Pattern: The recurring behavior or emotional tendency developed in response to unmet needs, offering a snapshot of how these patterns manifest and impact your adult life.

• Likely Unmet Needs: The specific needs that were not met, leading to the development of the pattern. Identifying these needs provides clarity on what drives the behavior, setting the stage for resolution.

• Compulsions: The drive to seek situations, relationships, or conditions in an attempt to meet those needs. These illustrate how one may act out or pursue behaviors to compensate for what was lacking.

• Aversions: Corresponding avoidance behaviors developed to protect oneself from further pain or perceived threats. They reveal how individuals sidestep certain experiences to prevent re-experiencing discomfort.

• Potential Triggers: Specific situations, interactions, or environments that activate the patterns and elicit conditioned responses. Triggers include sights, sounds, smells, or specific circumstances that evoke emotional intensity and can transport you back to distressing experiences.

• Likely Vice Expression: The habitual emotional or behavioral responses - such as fear, anger, shame, or bitterness - that emerge when an unmet need remains unresolved and perpetuate cycles of emotional distress.

This framework allows for a structured breakdown of reactive behaviors, enabling a transition toward more intentional choices. It draws on foundational principles from developmental psychology - particularly the understanding that unmet needs shape behavior - but applies them through the lens of sovereignty, lived experience, and belief architecture. While it may parallel elements of attachment theory or trauma response models, it diverges deliberately from diagnostic or typological systems. Rather than pathologizing behavior, this approach empowers individuals to decode and re-author their patterns with clarity and autonomy.


Note on associations:

The relationships mapped in Appendices 1 and 2, between patterns, needs, vices, compulsions, and triggers, represent likely associations derived from extensive field observation, iterative case application, and structural coherence testing over years of practice.

These mappings are not grounded in clinical trials or psychometric instruments, and they do not claim statistical or diagnostic authority. Rather, they serve as a functional scaffolding: a set of reflection and inference tools designed to support narrative exploration, deepen pattern recognition, and stimulate relevant associative thinking.

Their role is to assist individuals and practitioners in testing candidate patterns during belief work; not to assign definitive diagnoses, but to generate structurally plausible hypotheses. They are potentially diagnostic in orientation, but not prescriptive or specific in outcome.

Their value lies not in clinical universality, but in practical utility, interpretive flexibility, and the sovereignty they help restore through applied self-authorship.

Insecurity Management Strategies

In our efforts to manage insecurity, we often adopt destructive patterns of behavior that provide a false sense of control but ultimately worsen our mental and emotional state. These patterns may feel instinctive, yet they limit our ability to respond effectively to challenges and distort how we navigate relationships, environments, and internal struggles. Understanding these styles of insecurity is crucial to developing mental sovereignty, as they reveal the hidden forces that undermine your capacity for self-mastery and personal autonomy. By identifying and addressing these patterns, and recognizing the unmet needs driving them, you can reclaim control over your emotional resilience and empower yourself to engage with life from a place of clarity and intention.

There are four common styles of insecurity management that can be particularly harmful: Avoidance/Evasion, Perfectionism/Overcompensation, Blame/Externalization, and Sabotage. Each serves as a coping mechanism but ultimately hinders growth, emotional resilience, and self-mastery.

When confronted with the uncomfortable reality of our own failings, we often react in familiar, detrimental patterns. These reactions may provide temporary emotional relief, but they ultimately prevent us from taking ownership of our growth and hinder our progress toward mental sovereignty.

Recognizing which style of insecurity management you tend to lean on is a first step toward changing it. By understanding these common responses, you can begin to break free from disadvantageous behaviors, seek to meet the associated needs, and take responsibility for your own development; ultimately replacing them with healthier responses leading to more empowering outcomes.

Avoidance and Evasion

For some, insecurity leads to shutting down, mentally and emotionally withdrawing to avoid confronting difficult situations. This pattern is commonly associated with Avoidant and Disorganized attachment and may manifest as:

• Numbing yourself, zoning out, or mentally retreating to avoid feelings of insecurity.

• Isolating yourself from others, cutting off communication, and detaching emotionally as a defense mechanism.

While withdrawing provides temporary relief, it isolates you from supportive connections and stunts personal growth. This avoidance leads to stagnation, alienation, and deepening feelings of loneliness.

________________________________________

Defeat

• Narrative: “Not this again. I hate this pattern. I can’t seem to change it no matter what I do. This is agonizing, and I don’t want to look at it.”

• Impact: Overwhelmed by failure, you feel trapped in a cycle, leading to avoidance, stagnation, and self-loathing.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Support – A need for consistent encouragement and understanding from others, alleviating feelings of isolation and self-doubt.

• Clarity – Insight into personal patterns and strengths to enable growth and mitigate self-doubt.

• Acceptance – Recognition and self-acceptance that values one's worth beyond external achievements or perfection.

• Compassion – Self-kindness to counteract harsh self-judgment.

• Self-Respect – Building a self-assured identity that isn’t reliant on others’ validation.

• Compulsions: Seeking external validation to temporarily alleviate feelings of inadequacy or engaging in distraction behaviors to escape the feeling of failure.

• Aversions: Avoiding tasks, challenges, or self-reflection that might expose inadequacies, preferring to retreat into familiar routines or isolation.

• Potential Triggers: Criticism from others, encountering situations that require performance, or being compared to others.

• Likely Vice Expression: Anxiety, Shame, Worthlessness, Fear, Anguish

________________________________________

Rationalization

• Narrative: “This issue isn’t really a problem. Everyone has weaknesses, and this one doesn’t affect me much.”

• Impact: By downplaying the problem, you justify inaction, preventing meaningful change or growth.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – Maintaining a positive self-view by justifying shortcomings rather than confronting them.

• Acceptance – A need for acknowledgment of flaws and limitations without harsh self-judgment.

• Clarity – Honest self-awareness to recognize strengths and weaknesses with authenticity.

• Compassion – Self-kindness to face vulnerabilities, fostering openness to change.

• Understanding – Insight into one’s behaviors and choices, allowing for genuine personal growth.

• Compulsions: Seeking comfort in complacency or justifying behaviors to avoid the discomfort of growth and change.

• Aversions: Avoiding situations that challenge the status quo or require acknowledging areas of weakness.

• Potential Triggers: Confrontations where flaws are pointed out, situations demanding accountability, or being faced with others’ progress.

• Likely Vice Expression: Bitterness, Resentment, Frustration, Guilt, Disappointment

________________________________________

By-passing / Surrendered Acceptance

• Narrative: “I’m okay. I don’t need to focus on this. We’re all perfect the way we are, and these issues will resolve naturally.”

• Impact: This mindset promotes passivity, fostering the belief that no active effort is required. It creates a false sense of peace while neglecting meaningful self-improvement.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – The need for a genuine sense of self-worth, motivating active engagement rather than passivity.

• Closeness – A desire for meaningful intimacy and connection, which is compromised by withdrawal and avoidance.

• Support – Seeking encouragement and understanding, which could foster self-improvement rather than a passive acceptance.

• Clarity – A need for self-awareness and clear insight into personal situations, which is often evaded in favor of passive contentment.

• Compassion – An internal kindness that allows for growth and change, replacing the need to avoid discomfort.

• Compulsions: Seeking out environments or situations where you can remain passive and avoid confrontation, gravitating toward relationships that don’t challenge you to grow.

• Aversions: Avoiding conversations about personal development, conflict, or any situation that may demand effort or change, as they disrupt the illusion of peace.

• Potential Triggers: Confrontation, being asked to take responsibility, witnessing others’ growth or progress, feeling pressured to change, or experiencing failure.

• Likely Vice Expression: Sloth, Anxiety, Anguish, Resentment, Frustration

________________________________________

Comparative Disregard

• Narrative: “I have other qualities in abundance. This shortfall is minor in comparison, and I make up for it in other ways. No stress. It’s not a priority. Ya can’t have everything.”

• Impact: In this response, the individual minimizes the issue by focusing on their strengths. While this may temporarily avoid the problem, it can lead to complacency and an unwillingness to confront areas that need attention.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – Maintaining a positive self-view while acknowledging areas for improvement, rather than deflecting through comparisons.

• Acceptance – Acknowledgment of both strengths and flaws, reducing the urge to downplay weaknesses.

• Appreciation – Valuing inherent qualities without using them to overshadow other areas needing attention.

• Clarity – Honest self-assessment, allowing room for growth by directly confronting weaknesses.

• Community – A sense of connection that isn’t based on comparing oneself to others as a means of self-assurance.

• Compulsions: Highlighting personal strengths or achievements to deflect attention from areas needing improvement, seeking validation through comparison.

• Aversions: Avoiding direct engagement with weaknesses or areas of failure, as this would challenge the self-image that has been built around strengths.

• Potential Triggers: Situations where shortcomings are highlighted, being compared unfavorably to others, or facing criticism.

• Likely Vice Expression: Resentment, Disappointment, Frustration, Guilt, Anxiety

________________________________________

Avoidant Optimism

• Narrative: “Things will get better on their own, and I’ll handle it later.”

• Impact: This response involves deferring responsibility to the future, hoping that circumstances will improve without active intervention. This tendency leads to prolonged stagnation.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Clarity – The need for a realistic understanding of situations and feelings, often overlooked through deferred responsibility.

• Self-Respect – A desire for a grounded self-view that encourages facing challenges directly rather than postponing them.

• Support – Seeking encouragement from others to foster accountability and action, rather than remaining in a cycle of avoidance.

• Trust – Building confidence in oneself and in the process of facing challenges, reducing dependency on optimistic deferral.

• Companionship – A connection with others that mitigates the isolation caused by prolonged avoidance and disengagement.

• Compulsions: Engaging in distraction tactics (e.g., excessive screen time, overworking, or seeking temporary relief through other activities) as a way to avoid facing unresolved issues.

• Aversions: Avoiding planning, goal setting, or discussions that focus on addressing challenges directly, as these activities would require immediate action.

• Potential Triggers: Deadlines, increased responsibility, feeling pressured to take action, or seeing others achieving goals that you’ve deferred.

• Likely Vice Expression: Fear, Anxiety, Sloth, Disappointment, Frustration

Perfectionism and Overcompensation

Perfectionism often emerges from a fear of inadequacy and a deep need to control outcomes. This pattern is commonly associated with Anxious attachment, where fear of rejection and a desire for approval drive the pursuit of flawless performance, manifesting as:

• Overworking, constantly striving to achieve unrealistic standards, and never feeling "good enough" despite external success.

• Rigidly adhering to rules and expectations, avoiding any mistake or imperfection, and seeking validation through achievements.

While perfectionism may seem like a drive for excellence, it often masks underlying self-doubt and creates constant pressure, leaving little room for relaxation or true self-acceptance. This overcompensation hinders growth by reinforcing the belief that worth is earned only through performance, not through inherent value.

________________________________________

Perfectionism

• Narrative: “I need to fix this completely or it’s worthless. If I can’t do it perfectly, why even try?”

• Impact: The fear of imperfection can lead to either paralyzing inaction or an obsessive pursuit of unattainable ideals, blocking constructive progress.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – The need for a stable self-image that does not rely on flawless achievement.

• Competence – A desire to feel capable and skilled, which can be distorted into an obsessive need for excellence.

• Acceptance – The need for internal and external acknowledgment, valuing oneself beyond perfection.

• Support – Encouragement from others to pursue a balanced and realistic approach to goals.

• Clarity – Clear, realistic expectations to help counter the fear of falling short.

• Compulsions: Overworking or fixating on details in an attempt to perfect an outcome and prove self-worth.

• Aversions: Avoiding tasks where there is a risk of not achieving perfection, leading to procrastination or opting out entirely.

• Potential Triggers: Feedback or critique from others, high-stakes situations where performance is measured, or seeing others succeed.

• Likely Vice Expression: Fear, Anxiety, Shame, Frustration, Inferiority

________________________________________

Overcompensation

• Narrative: “If I double down on another area of strength, this problem won’t matter.”

• Impact: Instead of confronting the failing, a person may overcompensate in another area to offset feelings of inadequacy. This approach can provide temporary relief but ultimately reinforces avoidance of the core issue.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – Cultivating self-worth that doesn’t rely on excessive achievements or compensatory actions.

• Acceptance – Recognition and appreciation of oneself, including areas of vulnerability.

• Community – Seeking connection and belonging that is not conditional on performance or achievements.

• Support – Desire for encouragement in confronting challenges, reducing the need for overcompensation.

• Love – Affirmation and acceptance that reduces the need to constantly strive for external validation.

• Compulsions: Focusing intensely on excelling in specific areas as a way to distract from vulnerabilities, seeking approval or success in unrelated domains to feel secure.

• Aversions: Avoiding scenarios that would require addressing personal weaknesses or taking responsibility for failures.

• Potential Triggers: Feedback indicating a lack of skill or competence in certain areas, being placed in situations that highlight perceived inadequacies, or facing situations where support is not provided.

• Likely Vice Expression: Anger, Bitterness, Anxiety, Shame, Inferiority

Blame and Externalization (Projection)

When faced with failure or discomfort, some individuals deflect responsibility by projecting blame outward. This pattern is commonly associated with Anxious attachment, where fear of abandonment or inadequacy leads to externalizing blame, manifesting as:

• Pointing fingers at others, circumstances, or external factors rather than taking ownership of one’s actions or choices.

• Justifying personal failures or mistakes by shifting focus to the perceived wrongdoings or inadequacies of others.

While externalizing blame may protect the individual from immediate feelings of shame or inadequacy, it prevents true accountability and growth. This denial of personal responsibility stifles progress and perpetuates a cycle of helplessness and conflict.

________________________________________

Projection

• Narrative: “It’s not me - it’s them. I’m only struggling because of the people or circumstances around me.”

• Impact: Projection shifts blame onto others, externalizing responsibility for one’s own failings. This pattern avoids personal accountability, making it difficult to confront or address the underlying issue.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

◦ Self-Respect – A need for an internally grounded self-image that doesn’t rely on blaming others to maintain self-worth.

◦ Closeness – Desire for genuine intimacy and connection, often undermined by externalizing blame.

◦ Support – Encouragement to address personal challenges without projecting onto others, fostering a sense of shared understanding.

◦ Trust – Seeking reliability and emotional safety in relationships, which is often disrupted by projection.

◦ Compassion – Self-kindness to face personal struggles without deflecting accountability onto others.

• Compulsions: Attempting to control others’ behaviors or situations to feel secure, monitoring or criticizing those around you as a means of maintaining perceived control.

• Aversions: Avoiding self-reflection or accountability, fearing that acknowledging your own role might expose vulnerability or inadequacy.

• Potential Triggers: Conflicts with others, feeling undermined or unsupported, situations where expectations are not met, or when others behave unpredictably.

• Likely Vice Expression: Anger, Resentment, Anxiety, Bitterness, Frustration.

Sabotage

When insecurity overwhelms, it can escalate into self-sabotage. This pattern is associated with disorganized attachment and can manifest as:

• Lashing out to sabotage relationships or opportunities, creating chaos that results in rejection or isolation.

• Acting in ways that provoke others to push you away, reinforcing internal beliefs of unworthiness.

Sabotage cements insecurity in place and reinforces feelings of rejection and abandonment. It’s a self-fulfilling prophecy that confirms your belief that you’re unworthy of connection.

________________________________________

Destructive Actions

• Narrative: “I know this will ruin things, but I don’t deserve to have this anyway.”

• Impact: This behavior involves intentionally or subconsciously acting out in ways that disrupt relationships or opportunities. It often serves to confirm negative beliefs about oneself, making failure or rejection feel inevitable and deserved.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

o Self-Respect – A fundamental need to value oneself, often undermined by self-sabotage and the narrative of unworthiness.

o Belonging – The desire to feel accepted and included, frequently disrupted by actions that isolate and alienate.

o Love – Seeking emotional connection, which is feared and disrupted by destructive actions that push others away.

o Support – Encouragement and assistance during vulnerable moments, which are often resisted or sabotaged.

o Acceptance – Longing for acknowledgment and validation, which is internally rejected through self-destructive behaviors.

• Compulsions: Seeking validation through destructive means, such as picking fights, testing others' loyalty to extreme degrees, or engaging in risky behaviors that jeopardize relationships or opportunities.

• Aversions: Avoiding open communication, vulnerable discussions, or situations that may require self-reflection and accountability, as these would challenge the self-sabotaging narrative.

• Potential Triggers: Moments of intimacy, success, or positive feedback; fear of being seen as "enough"; situations where one is expected to accept support or trust others.

• Likely Vice Expression: Rage, Anguish, Guilt, Shame, Desperation

________________________________________

Chaos Creation

• Narrative: “If I create chaos, at least I have control over the outcome.”

• Impact: In this response, the individual disrupts stability by provoking conflicts or creating unpredictable situations. This can be an attempt to gain a sense of control or to pre-emptively end relationships, ensuring rejection occurs on their terms.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

o Self-Respect – The need to recognize one’s own worth, often disrupted by chaotic actions that reinforce instability and self-doubt.

o Stability – A need for consistency and predictability, which is frequently sabotaged by creating disorder.

o Trust – Confidence in oneself and in relationships, which is eroded by behaviors that cultivate chaos and insecurity.

o Closeness – The desire for intimacy, often undermined by actions that push others away, reinforcing isolation.

o Support – Seeking understanding and encouragement, which is frequently lost amid self-created turmoil.

• Compulsions: Stirring up drama or conflict to force a rupture in relationships or environments, as a way to align external circumstances with internal chaos.

• Aversions: Avoiding stable or predictable relationships and situations, as they challenge the belief that instability is inevitable or deserved.

• Potential Triggers: Experiencing stability, receiving care or acceptance, feeling seen or valued, or moments where one is required to maintain harmony.

• Likely Vice Expression: Bitterness, Anger, Resentment, Fear, Disgust

The Resilient Response: Confident Approach

Confident approach is the healthiest response, reflecting a willingness to engage with challenges, seek solutions, and persist despite past failures. It embodies an active, empowered stance rooted in self-belief and growth. While it is a positive and resilient style, it can also be driven by unmet needs and provide insights into one's internal landscape. The internal narrative may sound like "Alright! I’m willing to try again to address this weakness! If I don’t have the tools, I’ll find them. I believe in myself, even though I’ve failed before. I’ll make myself proud. I am the author of my future." This inspires willingness for taking on challenges, maintaining an optimistic and proactive mindset, and pursuing growth despite previous setbacks. There is a focus on self-improvement and resilience.

However, there are some potential hazards to be aware of:

Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – The desire to affirm one’s worth and capability, which drives a proactive pursuit of personal goals and self-belief.

• Autonomy – A need for independence and control over one's actions and decisions, reinforcing confidence in personal choices.

• Growth – The aspiration for continuous improvement, learning, and self-evolution, furling resilience and persistence.

• Clarity – A need for a clear vision of goals and the path forward, providing confidence in navigating challenges.

Compulsions:

• Overworking or pushing oneself beyond healthy limits to prove autonomy and competence.

• Seeking constant opportunities for growth and self-improvement, sometimes at the cost of rest or balance.

• Pursuing challenging situations repeatedly, even when they may not serve long-term well-being, to affirm self-trust and resilience.

Aversions:

• Avoiding situations that don’t offer clear opportunities for growth or autonomy, as they may feel limiting or stifling.

• Steering clear of environments where one's control or independence may be undermined, as these can trigger discomfort or frustration.

Potential Triggers:

• Situations where autonomy is restricted or where one feels micromanaged.

• Encounters where one's competence is questioned or where growth opportunities are blocked.

• Moments of failure or setbacks that challenge self-trust and the belief in one's capacity to overcome difficulties.

Likely Vice Expression (if unmet needs persist):

• Anxiety: Fear of losing autonomy or failing to meet growth expectations.

• Frustration: Feeling hindered or unable to make progress due to external limitations.

• Impatience: Struggling to accept setbacks or delays in achieving goals.

• Worthlessness: Feeling inadequate or doubting one’s competence when growth does not occur as planned.

Imprints of Childhood: Constraining Patterns in Adulthood

Our early life experiences shape our world-view, behaviors, and emotional responses. Sub-optimal parenting can leave lasting imprints that influence how we navigate adulthood, affecting our sense of autonomy, self-worth, and emotional resilience. Recognizing these patterns is the first step in reclaiming control and beginning the re-parenting process, where you actively heal and transform the parts of yourself that did not receive adequate support or nurturing during childhood.

________________________________________

Low Confidence

• Pattern: Finds it hard to feel secure in oneself, seeking external validation (compulsion) or avoiding challenges (aversion) due to fear of failure. This may stem from not being celebrated or encouraged as a child.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – The internal validation of worth, often diminished by the need for external approval.

• Appreciation – Acknowledgment and recognition for efforts, fostering self-worth when met.

• Acceptance – A need to be valued without judgment, building confidence and security in oneself.

• Support – Encouragement from others to face challenges, reinforcing resilience and risk-taking.

• Closeness – Emotional connection that nurtures self-assurance and a secure sense of belonging.

• Compulsions: Seeking constant reassurance, praise, or approval from others to feel valued.

• Aversions: Avoiding challenging tasks, unfamiliar situations, or feedback that could expose perceived inadequacies.

• Potential Triggers:

• Receiving critical feedback or negative evaluations

• Being compared to others or put in competitive situations

• Facing unfamiliar challenges or high-stakes tasks

• Not receiving recognition or praise for efforts

• Experiencing situations where others are praised while feeling overlooked

• Likely Vice Expression: Anxiety, Inferiority, Shame, Fear, Frustration

________________________________________

Dependence on Others

• Pattern: Finds it hard to make independent decisions or handle challenges without external guidance, possibly due to not being encouraged toward autonomy in childhood.

• Likely Unmet Needs

• Independence – The fundamental need to act autonomously, often stifled by dependency on authority figures.

• Self-Respect – Building confidence in one’s ability to make decisions, fostering a sense of competence.

• Support – External reassurance that encourages self-reliance rather than dependence.

• Acceptance – Validation and understanding from others that build confidence in one’s independence.

• Closeness – Emotional intimacy without over-reliance, allowing for healthy self-reliance within relationships.

• Compulsions: Clinging to authority figures, seeking advice excessively, or relying on others for decision-making to feel secure.

• Aversions: Avoiding situations where they must act alone or take full responsibility, as it triggers feelings of inadequacy or anxiety.

• Potential Triggers:

• Situations where independent decision-making is required

• Being left without guidance or support in challenging scenarios

• Expectations to take responsibility without external validation

• Instances where trusted figures are unavailable or unresponsive

• Pressure to make a significant or impactful choice alone

• Likely Vice Expression: Worthlessness, Guilt, Anguish, Desperation, Anxiety

________________________________________

Lack of Empathy

• Pattern: Finds it hard to emotionally connect with others or form meaningful relationships, likely due to a lack of empathetic response or understanding from caregivers during childhood.

• Likely Unmet Needs

• Empathy – A core need to connect with others emotionally, often unmet due to a lack of responsive caregiving.

• Understanding – Validation of personal experiences and feelings, fostering deeper connection with others.

• Closeness – Seeking meaningful relationships based on mutual emotional intimacy and understanding.

• Communication – Open expression of feelings, supporting emotional engagement and connection.

• Support – Emotional backing that fosters security in empathetic connections.

• Compulsions: Seeking out relationships where emotional needs are excessively catered to, trying to recreate the connection they didn’t receive.

• Aversions: Avoiding emotionally intense situations or deep conversations that require vulnerability or empathetic response, fearing discomfort or inability to connect.

• Potential Triggers:

• Being confronted with others' emotional needs or vulnerabilities

• Situations that require active listening or emotional engagement

• Perceived criticism about emotional unavailability or detachment

• Witnessing or being part of emotionally intense interactions

• Attempts by others to deepen the relationship or establish intimacy

• Likely Vice Expression: Bitterness, Anger, Resentment, Disappointment, Frustration

________________________________________

Fear of Challenges

• Pattern: Finds it hard to engage with difficulties, often feeling overwhelmed by obstacles. This may be the result of overprotection or insufficient encouragement to face challenges in childhood.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – Trust in one’s own abilities to handle obstacles, often compromised by overprotection or lack of empowerment.

• Growth – A need for personal development through experience, which is stifled by avoidance of challenges.

• Support – Reassurance and encouragement from others to face difficulties, fostering resilience.

• Clarity – A need for clear understanding when engaging with obstacles, helping to alleviate overwhelm.

• Acceptance – The desire to be embraced for one's efforts and strengths, reducing fear of judgment.

• Compulsions: Seeking easy wins or avoiding challenges altogether, preferring situations where they are guaranteed success or comfort.

• Aversions: Avoiding risk-taking, new ventures, or situations where they could potentially fail, as these scenarios amplify their sense of vulnerability.

• Potential Triggers:

• High-pressure situations with uncertain outcomes

• Opportunities that require risk-taking or stepping outside of comfort zones

• Perceived pressure to perform or achieve at a high standard

• Encounters with competitive or demanding environments

• Experiences of failure or being reminded of past mistakes

• Likely Vice Expression: Fear, Anxiety, Shame, Disappointment, Sloth

________________________________________

Lack of Practical Wisdom

• Pattern: Finds it hard to apply knowledge effectively or make informed decisions, possibly due to a lack of curiosity or support in problem-solving during childhood.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Clarity – A need for a clear understanding of how to apply knowledge practically, supporting confident decisions.

• Competence – Feeling capable and effective, often sought through validation when internal confidence is low.

• Understanding – Insight into one's own decision-making process, fostering genuine self-assurance.

• Support – Seeking guidance and reinforcement in problem-solving, building confidence in making decisions independently.

• Compulsions: Seeking validation through accumulating knowledge or consulting others to feel competent.

• Aversions: Avoiding decision-making scenarios where they must act without external input, fearing mistakes or criticism for their choices.

• Potential Triggers:

• Scenarios that demand quick or decisive action without external input

• Situations where knowledge is tested or where mistakes may have consequences

• Being expected to offer advice or take leadership roles

• Instances where previous knowledge is challenged or questioned

• Criticism regarding decision-making or problem-solving skills

• Likely Vice Expression: Anxiety, Guilt, Inferiority, Frustration, Disappointment

Emotional Detachment

• Pattern: Finds it hard to show compassion or form close emotional connections, potentially stemming from conditional love or emotional neglect during childhood.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Closeness – A need for genuine emotional connection and depth, often avoided due to fear of vulnerability.

• Compassion – Self-kindness and understanding that extend to others, fostering openness and empathy.

• Acceptance – A desire for non-judgmental support that allows for emotional expression without fear.

• Support – Reassurance needed to feel secure in emotional engagement, promoting safe vulnerability.

• Love – A core need for unconditional warmth and connection, enabling secure and genuine attachment.

• Also noteworthy: Connection, warmth.

• Compulsions: Engaging in superficial or transactional relationships where emotional depth is minimized to maintain a sense of safety.

• Aversions: Avoiding intimate or emotionally charged interactions, as they threaten to expose vulnerabilities or stir unresolved wounds.

• Potential Triggers:

• Attempts by others to initiate emotional closeness or intimacy

• Situations that require open vulnerability or expressions of compassion

• Confrontations about emotional unavailability or aloofness

• Interactions that remind of past experiences of conditional love or neglect

• Being in emotionally charged environments or discussions

• Likely Vice Expression: Bitterness, Anguish, Disgust, Resentment, Anxiety

________________________________________

Creative Stagnation

• Pattern: Finds it hard to express oneself authentically or feels blocked creatively, possibly due to a lack of encouragement or freedom to explore creativity in childhood.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Expression – A need for authentic self-expression, often blocked by fear of judgment or criticism.

• Freedom – Desire for autonomy in exploring creativity without restrictions, fostering true originality.

• Self-Respect – Valuing one’s unique ideas and contributions, helping to overcome fears of rejection.

• Closeness – Connecting with others in a supportive environment, encouraging genuine creative expression.

• Also note: Joy

• Compulsions: Seeking controlled or safe outlets for expression, where their creative efforts are likely to receive approval or minimal risk of judgment.

• Aversions: Avoiding any activity that requires authentic self-expression or puts them in a position of vulnerability, fearing rejection or criticism.

• Potential Triggers:

• Expectations to produce or express original ideas under scrutiny

• Opportunities that require stepping outside conventional or safe expressions

• Situations where self-expression is evaluated or judged

• Encounters with others who express creativity freely and confidently

• Pressure to present or perform in front of others

• Likely Vice Expression: Fear, Anguish, Shame, Inferiority, Frustration

________________________________________

Avoidance of Responsibility

• Pattern: Finds it hard to take accountability for actions, possibly due to overindulgence or lack of consequences in childhood.

• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Self-Respect – Trust in one’s own ability to handle accountability, often undermined by avoidance of responsibility.

• Competence – A need to feel capable and effective in fulfilling obligations, stifled by past overindulgence or lack of consequences.

• Integrity – A desire for consistency between actions and values, which supports accountable behavior.

Attachment Styles

Attachment theory posits that the way we relate to others in adulthood is profoundly shaped by our early attachment experiences with primary caregivers. These attachment styles - secure, anxious, avoidant, and disorganized - create a blueprint that influences our interactions, expectations, and emotional responses in relationships throughout life.

Attachment styles are not just psychological concepts - they are felt experiences, encoded deep in the right brain as implicit relational patterns. They influence our ability to connect with others, manage emotions, and navigate the complexities of intimacy and vulnerability.

Understanding these attachment patterns offers insight into the underlying unmet needs that drive behavior. By recognizing these needs, individuals can begin the process of transforming compulsions and aversions into healthier, more secure relational strategies. Engaging in re-parenting work and developing a coherent reality map helps transition from reactive, patterned responses into intentional, empowered ways of connecting with oneself and others.

Secure attachment is developed in environments where caregivers are consistently responsive, secure attachment forms a stable foundation, allowing individuals to engage in healthy relationships with confidence and resilience. People with secure attachment can express needs openly and trust others to respond positively.

Pre-Verbal Trauma Consequences

Pre-verbal trauma refers to the profound impact of experiences in infancy, before we have language to process them. These early traumas affect emotional regulation, self-worth, and the ability to form secure attachments throughout life. The unspoken experiences of neglect, abuse, and inconsistent care-giving create emotional wounds that continue to influence adulthood. Recognizing these patterns and their underlying unmet needs is essential for healing and reclaiming personal sovereignty.

________________________________________

Emotional Neglect

• Impact: Infants rely on caregivers for comfort and security. When left to cry without response or emotional connection, a deep sense of abandonment forms. This early neglect teaches the infant that their needs do not matter, leading to emotional dysregulation, insecure attachment, and a diminished sense of worth.


• Likely Unmet Needs:

◦ Safety – A foundational need for emotional security that was compromised by early neglect.

◦ Closeness – The need for meaningful emotional connection and validation from others, often sought excessively later in life.

◦ Compassion – A desire for understanding and empathy, fostering emotional self-worth and reducing dependency.

◦ Support – Reassurance and care from others to feel valued and recognized, reinforcing a sense of worth.

◦ Self-Respect – A need for internal self-worth that becomes weakened due to early neglect.

◦ Also note: Empathy, Nurturing


• Compulsions: Seeking excessive attention, affection, or validation from others to feel valued; pursuing relationships where emotional dependence is strong.


• Aversions: Avoiding intimacy or emotional vulnerability out of fear of further abandonment or rejection; withdrawing from relationships when feelings of neglect arise.


• Potential Triggers: Feeling ignored or unimportant in relationships or work; experiencing rejection, indifference, or neglect from others; physical or emotional isolation for extended periods.


• Likely Vice Expression: Worthlessness, Desperation, Anxiety, Shame, Frustration

Physical Neglect and Abuse

• Impact: Inadequate nutrition or exposure to physical harm disrupts normal development and weakens trust. The trauma of physical neglect or abuse leaves long-term imprints, often manifesting as self-neglect, low self-esteem, or maladaptive stress responses like self-harm.


• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Safety – The need for physical security, often jeopardized by past experiences of harm or neglect.

• Nourishment – A foundational need for physical care and sustenance, undermined by inadequate provision in childhood.

• Stability – The desire for a secure, predictable environment, helping to counteract experiences of physical neglect.

• Support – A need for reassurance and reliable care from others, restoring a sense of physical and emotional security.

• Self-Respect – Confidence in one’s right to physical well-being, often compromised by past abuse or neglect.

• Also note: shelter, touch.


• Compulsions: Seeking physical comfort through unhealthy means, such as overeating or hoarding; clinging to protective environments or people to feel secure.


• Aversions: Avoiding situations that feel physically unsafe; rejecting care from others due to distrust; avoiding environments or relationships involving authority or control.


• Potential Triggers: Situations involving food scarcity; witnessing violence; medical procedures; encounters with authority figures; unwelcome physical touch or feeling physically trapped.


• Likely Vice Expression: Fear, Rage, Anguish, Disgust, Bitterness

Inconsistent / Unpredictable Care-giving

• Impact: When caregivers are emotionally unavailable or frequently changing, it fosters a sense of instability and unreliability in emotional connections. This unpredictability leads to fear of abandonment, difficulty trusting others, and a persistent sense of insecurity.


• Likely Unmet Needs:

• Consistency – A fundamental need for reliable and predictable emotional responses, fostering trust and emotional security.

• Trust – The ability to rely on others and feel secure in relationships, often destabilized by early unpredictability.

• Stability – Desire for a predictable environment and relationships that help alleviate fears of abandonment.

• Closeness – Emotional connection that offers a sense of security, reducing the urge to seek constant reassurance.

• Support – Reliable encouragement from others that reinforces a stable and secure sense of self.

• Also note: security.


• Compulsions: Clinging to relationships or routines that offer a sense of security; seeking constant reassurance from partners or friends.


• Aversions: Avoiding deep relationships out of fear of abandonment; resisting change (e.g., new jobs or relocations) due to fear of instability.


• Potential Triggers: Starting or ending relationships; emotional unavailability from partners; changes in job or location; interactions with authority figures.


• Likely Vice Expression: Anxiety, Anger, Intolerance, Disappointment, Resentment.

◦ Resentment – Frustration about being physically limited or dependent on others.

◦ Desperation – The sense of hopelessness or urgency in trying to regain a sense of normalcy or control over one’s life.
